# Game Theory of Listings

## Abstract

This paper argues that many of crypto's recurring structural failures originate in the **game theory of listings**. Any exchange, protocol, or listing committee that bears reputational cost, capital cost, or explicit gatekeeping power cannot list based on actual realized market demand. It can only list based on **perceived interest**: predicted user demand, expected volume, modeled safety, and social proof. That distinction is the root of a wide range of industry distortions.

The central framework is simple. On one axis sits **perceived interest**. On the other sits **actual interest**. The latter can only be known after the market exists; before that point, it is fundamentally uncertain. Curated systems must therefore rely on proxies. They look for metrics, narratives, traction signals, deposits, and reputational assurances. Projects respond by optimizing for those signals. The result is a market that increasingly rewards listability rather than real evolutionary discovery.

This logic helps explain phenomena such as low-float high-FDV launches, metric gaming, exchange listing deposits, meme-coin migration, and the growing disconnect between token creation and serious market access. Zero-cost permissionless systems like early DEX environments preserve more noise and more failure, but they also preserve the possibility of discovering the assets that no central authority could have rationally selected in advance.

The implication for Vibe is strategic. If the industry's missing layer is permissionless perpetual access, then perps must be brought to the **zero perceived interest arena**: the trenches where future unicorns begin. Vibe's role is not merely to list more assets. It is to restore evolutionary openness to derivative markets by making perpetual access possible before centralized or high-cost systems are willing to validate a token.
