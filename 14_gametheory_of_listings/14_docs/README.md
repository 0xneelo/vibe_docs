# Game Theory of Listings

## Paper Overview

This paper argues that one of crypto's deepest structural problems comes from the way markets are listed. Any exchange, protocol, or committee that faces meaningful listing cost, reputational exposure, or stake-based gatekeeping cannot optimize for **actual interest**. It can only optimize for **perceived interest**.

That difference sounds subtle, but it explains a large share of industry behavior:

- why centralized venues over-select for safe, obvious listings
- why projects game metrics to appear list-worthy
- why low-float high-FDV launches emerged so easily
- why meme markets became the last venue for retail-style discovery
- why there is a massive disconnect between token creation and perp access

The paper frames listing design as a game-theoretic problem. Curated systems try to protect users and preserve reputation, but in doing so they interfere with the natural evolutionary path of markets. Zero-cost permissionless systems do the opposite: they allow more noise, but they also preserve the possibility of discovering assets whose future interest was impossible to predict in advance.

## Core Thesis

- Listings are not neutral infrastructure. They are market-shaping filters.
- Any listing system with cost or curation must act on predicted demand, not actual realized demand.
- That prediction pressure causes projects to optimize for signals, optics, and perceived safety.
- Zero-cost permissionless systems preserve more evolutionary noise, but also more genuine discovery.
- Vibe matters because it extends market access into the part of crypto where perceived interest is lowest and future upside is hardest to predict.
