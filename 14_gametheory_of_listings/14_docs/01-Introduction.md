# Introduction

Crypto likes to talk about market efficiency, decentralization, and permissionless innovation. But one of the industry's deepest bottlenecks is much simpler: **who gets listed, where, and under what conditions**.

Listings are not passive infrastructure. They are selection mechanisms. They decide which assets gain visibility, which assets receive liquidity, which assets gain legitimacy, and which assets are left in the shadows. Because of that, listing systems shape the trajectory of the entire industry.

The common mistake is to think of listings as a binary question of centralized versus decentralized. The more important distinction is structural: **zero-cost permissionless** versus **costly or curated**.

If a system imposes high stake requirements, reputational review, security deposits, or explicit committee judgment, then it is no longer allowing pure market evolution. It is introducing a filter. That filter may be understandable, rational, and even protective. But it still changes outcomes.

This paper explores that logic through a simple claim: listing venues with meaningful cost or curation must optimize for **perceived interest**, not **actual interest**. Since actual interest can only be observed after the market has been allowed to exist, curated systems are forced to rely on predictions and proxies. That creates powerful second-order effects throughout crypto.

The result is a landscape where:

- projects optimize for appearances and metrics
- venues avoid assets that might embarrass them
- retail discovery gets pushed into the least curated corners
- the future winners of the industry often begin where no serious venue is willing to look

The rest of this paper develops that framework and shows why Vibe's long-term role is to bring perpetuals to exactly that neglected edge.
